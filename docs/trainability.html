Why it's easier to train

Looped, recurrent, and weight-tied Transformers add depth without adding parameters — but they are notoriously hard to optimize. The two-pass additive-routing design sidesteps those difficulties by construction. Here is the failure-mode-by-failure-mode comparison, with citations.

Full doc & refs → Results & architecture

The design, in one line

Not a deep recurrence — a fixed, twice-run trunk with a gated additive prior.

Run the shared-weight trunk exactly twice. Pass 1 caches the RoPE'd (q₁,k₁) at a few source layers; each pass-2 layer adds a causal additive attention-logit prior g·q₁k₁ᵀ, with a per-layer gate g=softplus(gate_logit) initialized near zero. Fully differentiable, ordinary backprop — no BPTT-through-many-steps, no fixed-point solve, no halting.

Failure mode → how this design avoids it

Traditional looped / recurrent / weight-tied
Two-pass additive routing
halting
ACT / halting is unstable. Universal Transformers decide per-position depth with Adaptive Computation Time; Graves: “quite sensitive to… τ… not obvious how to choose a good value.” PonderNet is “challenging and sensitive to… the KL divergence” weight; adaptive halting starves deep steps of gradient. Graves 2016; Dehghani 2019; Mohtashami 2023
no halting
The loop count is a fixed constant of 2. There is no halting distribution to train, no time-penalty to tune, and every pass gets full gradient signal.
BPTT
Deep BPTT vanishes/explodes. Unrolled recurrence has “the vanishing and the exploding gradient problems,” needing gradient clipping and soft constraints. Pascanu, Mikolov & Bengio 2013
2-step graph
The backward graph spans only 2 passes. With the gate near zero at init, training starts essentially identical to a standard Transformer — a well-conditioned landscape.
truncation
Activation memory forces truncated BPTT → biased gradients. TBPTT “is biased, so… does not benefit from the convergence guarantees,” can “diverge,” and its length is hard to pick. A recent recurrent-depth LLM backprops through only the last k=8 steps. Tallec & Ollivier 2017; Aicher 2019; Geiping 2025
exact, bounded
Fixed depth 2 ⇒ bounded memory ⇒ no truncation. The full 2-pass graph is differentiated exactly — no truncation bias, no truncation-length hyperparameter.
fixed point
DEQ equilibria are unstable. Implicit models “suffer from unstable convergence… and lack guarantees that a solution exists”; stability is set by the Jacobian and must be engineered via spectral/Jacobian regularization or monotone-operator constraints. Bai 2019/2021; Winston & Kolter 2020; TorchDEQ 2023
no solve
No fixed point is ever solved. It is a plain twice-run forward pass — no equilibrium to converge, no existence question, nothing to spectrally regularize.
sequential
Exact recurrence is sequential and does not parallelize well over the loop, hurting hardware utilization.
parallel
Fully parallel over the sequence. The prior is an additive logit bias via width-2D concatenated Q/K through fused FlexAttention — no step-by-step recurrence.

The key lever: a near-zero-gate warm start

The gate g = softplus(gate_logit) starts near zero, so at initialization the routing term is off and the model is ~identical to a standard Transformer. Training begins on the same well-conditioned landscape ordinary Transformers already train well on, and the prior is ramped in smoothly as the gate grows — a homotopy from “standard Transformer” to “looped model,” rather than optimizing the full looped objective cold. The same zero-init-gate principle stabilizes training in ReZero and Flamingo. None of the deep-recurrence approaches has an analogous free “start as a standard Transformer” initialization.

The honest tradeoff

Trainability is bought by giving up depth's ceiling.

Deep recurrence's main payoff is test-time compute scaling — unrolling to more iterations at inference than seen in training (Geiping et al. 2025 report gains “up to a computation load equivalent to 50 billion parameters”). The two-pass model's loop count is fixed at 2, so it gets none of that — and we verified directly that running >2 passes at inference only degrades quality (the test-time pass-scaling negative result). It also costs ~2.08× the training FLOPs and is, by design, less expressive than a deep loop.

So the claim is narrow: for a fixed, small number of passes, this design captures a useful slice of “looped” behavior while staying as trainable as a standard Transformer — trading the ceiling of deep recurrence for the floor of its optimization difficulty.
